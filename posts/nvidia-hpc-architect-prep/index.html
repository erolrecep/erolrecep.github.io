Given the nature of this role, you’re likely to encounter behavioral questions that assess both your technical capabilities and your interpersonal skills. Since this position requires hands-on technical expertise in HPC (High Performance Computing) and GPU-accelerated computing, and also emphasizes communication, teamwork, and problem-solving, you should prepare for questions that cover a variety of areas.

Here are some potential categories and questions you might expect:

1. Technical Problem Solving and Expertise

These questions will test your ability to troubleshoot and provide solutions in complex and dynamic environments.

Examples:

  • Tell me about a time when you had to troubleshoot a performance issue in an HPC cluster. What steps did you take to identify and solve the problem?
  • Describe a challenging technical project you worked on involving large-scale GPU-accelerated systems. How did you approach it, and what was the outcome?
  • Can you explain a situation where you had to optimize the performance of an HPC system? What tools did you use, and what impact did your changes have?

What to focus on: Show your deep technical knowledge, especially related to GPUs, parallel file systems, and resource management. Highlight how you’ve used analytical thinking to solve problems and improve system performance.

2. Collaboration and Teamwork

Since the role involves working with various teams (e.g., systems engineers, machine learning researchers), they’ll want to assess how well you collaborate and communicate with others.

Examples:

  • Give an example of a time when you had to collaborate with a cross-functional team to deploy an HPC solution. How did you ensure alignment between technical and non-technical team members?
  • Have you ever worked with a research team to optimize workflows for a deep learning project? What challenges did you face and how did you overcome them?
  • Tell me about a situation where you had to communicate complex technical concepts to non-technical stakeholders. How did you ensure they understood?

What to focus on: Showcase your teamwork and communication skills. Emphasize how you’ve worked with diverse teams and how you adjust your communication style based on your audience (e.g., engineers vs. researchers).

3. Leadership and Initiative

In a senior role, you’ll likely be expected to take initiative and potentially lead projects or mentor others. These questions will test your leadership and decision-making abilities.

Examples:

  • Describe a time when you had to take ownership of a large, complex HPC project. How did you ensure its success?
  • Tell me about a time when you identified an area for improvement in an HPC system or workflow. What steps did you take to implement change?
  • Have you mentored junior engineers or researchers? Can you describe an experience where you helped someone improve their technical abilities or solve a problem?

What to focus on: Illustrate your ability to lead, mentor, and drive initiatives. Focus on your decision-making process, the challenges you faced, and how your actions contributed to success.

4. Adaptability and Multitasking

The job description mentions that the environment is dynamic and you’ll need to juggle multiple projects. Expect questions that probe how you handle such scenarios.

Examples:

  • Tell me about a time when you had to balance multiple competing priorities in a high-pressure environment. How did you manage your time and resources?

  • How do you stay organized when working on multiple projects, especially with tight deadlines?

What to focus on: Provide examples that highlight your ability to prioritize tasks, stay organized, and deliver high-quality work under pressure.

What to focus on: Highlight your ability to listen, mediate, and find win-win solutions that keep the team moving forward. Showcase your interpersonal skills and professionalism.

STAR Response:

  • Situation:
    In a previous project, I was tasked with optimizing a deep learning model training process that was running on a single GPU, but was taking an unacceptably long time due to the scale of the data and complexity of the model. The goal was to reduce the training time significantly, ideally allowing the team to iterate faster on experiments and achieve faster time-to-solution.

  • Task:
    My task was to optimize the model’s training process by profiling and parallelizing the code to leverage multiple GPUs, ideally transforming the existing code so that it could scale efficiently across a multi-GPU setup without introducing significant overhead or synchronization issues.

  • Action:
    To solve this problem, I took a step-by-step approach:

    1. Profiling and Benchmarking: I first profiled the original code to understand the bottlenecks in terms of CPU/GPU utilization, memory usage, and computation time. I identified that the data loading was one bottleneck and that the model training loop had inefficiencies in how gradients were being aggregated.

    2. Parallelization Strategy: Based on the profiling results, I decided to employ data parallelism to split the training data across multiple GPUs. Each GPU would process a subset of the data and compute gradients in parallel, which would then be synchronized across the GPUs.

    3. Implementation: I refactored the code to use NVIDIA’s NCCL (NVIDIA Collective Communications Library) for efficient communication and gradient synchronization between GPUs. I also implemented mixed precision training to reduce memory overhead and increase throughput, which worked well with the multi-GPU setup.

    4. Distributed Training Setup: To further improve performance, I integrated the model with a distributed training framework like Horovod, which uses ring-based algorithms for efficient data distribution and gradient synchronization in distributed multi-GPU setups. I also optimized the I/O pipeline by using NVIDIA DALI (Data Loading Library) to speed up the data pipeline and reduce CPU-GPU communication overhead.

    5. Testing and Optimization: After applying these changes, I ran multiple test cases, comparing the performance of the single GPU vs. multi-GPU setups. I made adjustments to batch sizes and fine-tuned the distributed training hyperparameters to ensure that the scaling across GPUs did not result in diminishing returns or excessive overhead.

  • Result:
    The changes led to a significant reduction in training time—about 70% faster compared to the single-GPU version. Additionally, the refactored system was highly scalable; when we added more GPUs to the cluster, the training time decreased further with minimal impact on the communication overhead. The final system allowed the research team to run more experiments per day, speeding up their model development cycle and ultimately contributing to faster project delivery. The success of this optimization also led to the adoption of similar parallelization techniques for other projects across the team.

    • Profiling the code to identify bottlenecks.
    • Selecting a parallelization strategy (data parallelism in this case).
    • Implementing multi-GPU techniques (using NCCL and Horovod).
    • Optimizing the I/O and memory management (e.g., using DALI).
  • Result: Quantify the impact of your work. How much did performance improve? Was it scalable? What benefits did the team see as a result?

A feedback about the interview process for a position og HPC _ I’m on the HPC admin side and do a fair amount of interviews.

Usually they would have been given one or two basic interviews by a recruiter and/or project manager before they get to me. I will quiz them on products or technologies they have in their resume that may be relevant to the position as well as general Linux knowledge and concepts and try to assess what their skill level is and ability to pick up new skills quickly as well as personality. Nobody will know everything we need, everyone will need some level of training.

If I think they are a good candidate then I’ll pass them on to do a group interview with the team in person. In the team interview each candidate gets asked the same set of questions, nobody gets extra hard questions or an unfair advantage it’s all the same. Afterwards the team reviews how the candidate did on each of the questions and discusses their prospective and/or concerns. If the group says yes then its out of our hands and up to HR to make an offer.

No Leet code questions anywhere in the process.

_

For HPC software engineering, you’ll have to know the basics of working with scientific data structures, parallel debugging, parallel profiling, and reasoning about the results of profiling/debugging. A good interviewer will ask open-ended questions to assess your approach to problem solving, not necessarily whether you know the bubble sort algorithm. A few example questions off the dome:

Implement a basic GEMM. Then suggest some optimizations to make it faster.

Someone files an issue saying "Your app is slow." How do you troubleshoot this? What tools, what data, etc do you use?

What can you tell me about roofline?

Expect questions that revolve around things like memory, network I/O, scalability, algorithmic intensity, and perhaps hardware topics related to these. Depending on the seniority of the position, you may be expected to ask clarifying questions before answering. Or perhaps you may not be expected to come up with an answer at all, but instead show that you would be able to plan out a way to figuring it out if you had to.

Good luck!
